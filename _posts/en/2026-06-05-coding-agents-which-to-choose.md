In 2024 coding assistants were a novelty. Today they are part of the stack. And the choice of agent affects your team's productivity more than most technical leads calculate when they sign the invoice.

The 2026 landscape is broad. Claude Code for teams that live in the terminal and want an agent with deep context. Cursor for those who live in an IDE. Codex CLI and Aider for automated pipelines. Windsurf, Roo Code and others for more specific cases. On paper they all do the same thing; in practice they behave differently depending on the language, the size of the codebase and the team's workflow.

## How to decide

These are the four criteria we apply with our clients when it is time to pick an agent for a development team:

- **Useful context window.** Benchmarks quote huge numbers. The real context an agent can reason well over is smaller. Test it against your own repository.
- **Fit with the current workflow.** An agent that breaks your team's habits takes months to adopt. One that plugs into the terminal or editor they already use installs itself in a day.
- **Cost per developer and data control.** Seat subscriptions, metered tokens, retention policies. Three factors to calculate before signing, not after.
- **Real autonomous-agent capability.** Autocomplete and agent are two different products. If your team is going to delegate long-running tasks you need an agent. If they only write code inline, a good autocomplete is enough.

## How we do it

We pilot with two or three people from the team for two weeks. We measure speed, quality and satisfaction. With data in hand, the general rollout gets decided on judgment.

Choosing well is easy when you have tested. And testing well is easy when someone has already gone through the comparison before.
